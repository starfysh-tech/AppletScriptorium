From Prediction to PRO-Diction Models: Incorporating Patient-Reported Outcomes Into Predictive Oncology

September 24, 2025

Jordan S. Goldstein, MD, MS, and Ash A. Alizadeh, MD, PhD
Share on

Key Points:

  • Integrating patient-reported outcomes (PROs) into machine learning (ML) risk models can enhance and humanize personalized oncology predictions by capturing patient symptoms, quality of life (QOL), and physical function.
  • Standardizing PRO collection, integrating electronic health records (EHRs), and adhering to established guidelines for PROs and artificial intelligence (AI) are methods to overcome current clinical and administrative challenges and ensure robust, generalizable risk prediction models.
  • Thoughtful incorporation of PROs into dynamic risk models can bridge data-driven insights and patient-centered care to improve clinical and regulatory decision-making.

Question: Where do you see the role of or potential for integrating PROs into ML models—for example, for predicting mortality risk or risk of relapse? Are there any standardized guidelines for their integration?

Answer: Prediction drives cancer treatment decisions. The emergence of digital medicine and AI is transforming predictive oncology, a burgeoning field that uses sophisticated models to personalize care.1 Historically, prognostic models in oncology relied on limited clinical or molecular parameters. ML methods enhance risk prediction models by incorporating large-scale multiomic, radiologic, pathologic, or EHR data.2 Although these advances can accelerate precision oncology, current models often exclude the patient experience, limiting truly personalized treatment and prognostication.

Herein lie the pros of PROs, which directly capture symptoms or QOL metrics from the patient without clinician interpretation and are increasingly incorporated in clinical trials and regulatory decision-making.3 When collected with validated instruments, PROs provide clinically valuable information—disease-related symptoms, adverse drug events, or physical and cognitive function—that may not otherwise be captured by clinicians or conventional efficacy and safety measures.4 Integrating PROs into routine cancer care improves QOL and survival outcomes by enhancing symptom management and disease recognition.5,6 Furthermore, PRO measures themselves predict disease recurrence and survival.

ML risk prediction models and PROs are new tools in oncology with limited overlap thus far in spite of their potential (Table).7-15 By incorporating data on cancer-related symptoms, QOL, and mental and physical function, PROs improve short-term mortality prediction, which oncologists often overestimate.7,9,16 Improved mortality prediction can increase palliative care use and promote goal-concordant end-of-life care. However, end-of-life risk models currently use various PRO measurement tools, complicating their implementation (Table).7-15 Beyond short-term mortality, PROs in ML models also predict disease recurrence, treatment toxicities, postoperative complications, and care utilization (Table).7-15 ML models can also forecast PROs themselves, such as symptoms or QOL trajectories.15

Electronic PROs (ePROs) enable efficient, real-time capture of patient experiences, facilitating timely communication with physicians and incorporation into ML models. Emerging AI technologies, particularly large language models, may allow PRO extraction from unstructured clinical texts. Dynamic risk models that combine pretreatment clinical features with pathologic, radiologic, or molecular response to forecast treatment response, prognosis, and mortality promise a new era of predictive oncology (Figure).17 Integrating ePROs allows more precise dynamic outcome predictions and the forecasting of toxicity and QOL over time, enabling patient-centered decision-making.18

Cancer-specific ML models that incorporate PROs may offer the most useful predictions and interventions. For certain hematologic cancers, such as myeloproliferative neoplasms or indolent lymphomas, in which oncologists prioritize treatments that improve QOL, ML models that forecast PROs may be well-suited. Many solid tumors present disease-specific symptoms based on initial or metastatic cancer sites, which accurately predict disease recurrence.

To implement PROs in ML models, several challenges must be addressed. Despite years of evidence showing PROs benefit cancer symptom control, treatment success, and survival, EHRs often do not routinely collect these data. This limits their practical use in risk models and hinders the development of data-intensive ML methods. Fragmented data collection and inconsistent PRO instruments prevent data harmonization and generalizability of ML models.

Standardization is key to unlocking the potential of PROs in dynamic ML models. Clinicians should routinely collect ePROs in oncologic care and integrate them seamlessly into the EHR to garner large data sets. PRO data collection must be standardized for each clinical context. Researchers should study and use validated PRO measures to ensure consistency and comparability. Standardized approaches are needed for data harmonization and handling of missing data, which can occur because of systematic reasons or patient illness. ML methods must handle longitudinal PRO data and be explainable to drive clinician uptake and implement relative values for shared decision-making. Finally, researchers should design high-quality PRO and ML studies by following guidelines, such as SPIRIT-PRO and SPIRIT-AI, to clearly define research questions and interventions.19,20 External validation on independent, prospective data sets will determine generalizability.

PROs and risk prediction models are synergistic tools poised to transform personalized oncology care. Leveraging PROs in dynamic prediction frameworks can improve decision-making by humanizing AI and supporting multiple stakeholders. Standardizing PRO collection, integrating EHRs, and designing robust, transparent studies are essential for successful implementation. Prioritizing model interpretability and clearly communicating rationale will foster clinician trust, patient engagement, and regulatory acceptance. Thoughtfully integrating PROs into predictive oncology bridges the gap between data-driven insights and patient-centered care.
